Breathing in the Metropolis
Mapping Air Pollution in NYC

The New York City Community Air Survey (NYCCAS) is the largest ongoing urban air monitoring program of any U.S. city. It was established by the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene in 2007, and began collecting data in December 2008 for evaluating the air quality across NYC.

They chose about 100 street light poles to set the monitors which includes the routine sites spread around the city and environmental justice sites near potentially high-emission locations. The monitors will be strategically mounted at a height of 10-12 feet above the ground on road poles. These collection devices will serve the purpose of gathering various pollutants throughout the year.

Specifically, PM2.5, NO2, and NOx will be continuously collected, while O3 collection will be limited to the summer months. This seasonal differentiation is primarily due to the significant contribution of anthropogenic sources, such as emissions of NOx and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) , which lead to the formation of photochemical smog and secondary ozone production in the presence of intense solar radiation and higher temperatures.

From 2013 to 2021, the latest year of available data, the annual mean of three leading air pollutants decrease over the years:
PM2.5 (Fine particles) - 24.4%
NO2 (Nitrogen Dioxide) - 16.5%
NOx (Nitric Oxide) - 31.7

The Air Quality in NYC is getting better every year due to policies to enhance cleanup by increasing the area of Green Spaces and reduce emissions from transportation by supporting the development of Clean Energy and Bike Sharing.

According to Srbinovska's (2020) research, a green area reduces the amount of PM2.5 by an average of 25%. Trees can also remove up to 15% of ozone and 8% of NO2 from the air in areas with complete tree coverage (D.J., 2002).
